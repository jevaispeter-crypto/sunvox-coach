# 01\. Sound, Signal, Levels, and Phase

## Core idea

Audio is not “magic.”
It is a signal moving through a system.

To make good production decisions, the student must understand four things:

* frequency
* level
* waveform
* phase

These are not abstract physics facts.
They directly control:

* pitch
* loudness
* timbre
* clarity



## What the student must understand

* frequency
* amplitude / level
* waveform
* harmonics
* decibels
* phase
* polarity
* cancellation



## Why it matters

Weak production often comes from:

* treating level as if it were linear
* boosting things without understanding frequency balance
* layering sounds that cancel each other
* hearing “thinness” or “mud” without knowing the cause

Strong production usually has:

* controlled levels
* clear frequency balance
* awareness of phase problems
* intentional tone shaping



## Hearing model



\## Practical interpretation



Most production problems fall into one of three categories:



\- level problems → something is too loud or too quiet

\- tonal problems → frequency balance is wrong

\- phase problems → signals interfere or weaken each other



Before using any tool, identify which category you are dealing with.



Examples:



\- a sound feels buried → level or masking

\- a layered sound feels weak → possible phase issue

\- a mix feels muddy → frequency balance, not volume



### Frequency

Higher frequency = higher perceived pitch

Lower frequency = lower perceived pitch



### Level

More level = louder sound

But loudness is perceived roughly logarithmically, not linearly.



### Waveform and harmonics

A note is not only its fundamental.
Its harmonics shape the timbre.

This is why two sounds can play the same pitch but feel completely different.



### Phase

Two similar signals can reinforce each other or cancel each other depending on timing and polarity.

If timing differs slightly:

* comb filtering can appear
* tone becomes hollow or smeared

If polarity is reversed in an extreme case:

* major cancellation can occur



## Production heuristics

* First ask whether a problem is tonal, level-based, or phase-based
* If a layered sound feels weak, check alignment before adding EQ
* Use dB changes modestly and compare in context
* Treat phase as a clarity issue, not just a “technical” issue



## Core drills

### Drill 1 — Level perception

Raise a sound slightly, then lower it slightly.

Goal:
hear how small dB changes alter perceived balance



### Drill 2 — Harmonic identity

Compare two sounds playing the same note but with different timbres.

Goal:
hear how waveform / harmonics change identity



### Drill 3 — Phase sensitivity

Layer two similar sounds and offset one slightly.

Goal:
hear hollowing, smearing, or cancellation



### Drill 4 — Polarity check

Duplicate a sound and reverse polarity if possible.

Goal:
hear how similar material can partially or strongly cancel



## Contrast prompts

Ask:

* is this a frequency problem or a level problem?
* does the layered version sound fuller or weaker?
* where does the tone lose solidity?
* which sound has more harmonic richness?



## Common mistakes

* assuming louder = better
* using large level moves too early
* ignoring phase when layering
* treating tone problems as EQ problems only
