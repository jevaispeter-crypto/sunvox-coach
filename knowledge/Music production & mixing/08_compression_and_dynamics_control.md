# 05\. Compression and Dynamics Control

## Core idea

Compression is not automatically “professional.”
Compression is a control tool.

It changes:

* dynamic range
* transient emphasis
* sustain behavior
* perceived stability

Good compression has a reason.
Bad compression only makes things flatter or more confusing.



## What the student must understand

* dynamic range
* threshold
* ratio
* attack
* release
* makeup gain
* transient control
* overcompression



## Why it matters

Weak production often comes from:

* compressing because “that’s what mixing does”
* flattening material without listening for side effects
* making everything equally dense
* using makeup gain to confuse loudness with improvement
* setting attack / release blindly

Strong production usually has:

* compression with a purpose
* preserved musical movement
* controlled peaks where needed
* intentional transient shaping



## Hearing model

### Threshold

The threshold decides when compression begins.



### Ratio

The ratio decides how strongly peaks above the threshold are reduced.



### Attack

Fast attack:

* catches transients quickly
* can soften punch

Slower attack:

* lets the front of the sound through
* can preserve or increase punch



### Release

Release controls how quickly the compressor lets go.

Bad release settings can sound:

* jumpy
* smeared
* distorted
* unnaturally pumping



### Makeup gain

A compressed sound often becomes quieter.
Makeup gain raises the overall level afterward.

This can trick the ear into preferring compression just because it is louder.



\## When to use compression



Use compression when you need to:



\- control peaks (too dynamic)

\- stabilize a performance

\- shape transients (more punch or softer attack)

\- increase sustain

\- make a sound sit more consistently in the mix



Do NOT use compression if:



\- the problem is arrangement or balance

\- the sound already feels stable



## Production heuristics

* Compress only when you can name the problem
* Level-match before judging whether compression helped
* Use attack / release to shape behavior, not just to “set something”
* If the sound lost life, back off
* Listen for distortion, pumping, or loss of low-end weight



## Core drills

### Drill 1 — Control vs flattening

Compress a sound gently, then heavily.

Goal:
hear when control becomes damage



### Drill 2 — Attack contrast

Use a faster and then slower attack on a drum or pluck sound.

Goal:
hear transient softening vs punch retention



### Drill 3 — Release contrast

Try short vs longer release times.

Goal:
hear smooth recovery vs obvious pumping / instability



### Drill 4 — Loudness bias check

Level-match compressed and uncompressed versions.

Goal:
judge compression by behavior, not by loudness



## Contrast prompts

Ask:

* what exact problem is this compressor solving?
* did the sound become more stable or just smaller?
* did I improve punch or remove it?
* am I preferring the louder version?



## Common mistakes

* compressing by default
* using too much makeup gain
* crushing transients unintentionally
* ignoring distortion side effects
* setting threshold, ratio, attack, and release without a listening goal
