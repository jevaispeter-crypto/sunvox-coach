# 04\. EQ and Frequency Balance

## Core idea

EQ is not “make it sound better.”
EQ is frequency problem-solving.

The most useful EQ decisions usually do one of three things:

* remove unnecessary energy
* create separation
* reshape emphasis



## What the student must understand

* frequency balance
* masking
* cuts vs boosts
* broad vs narrow moves
* tonal shaping
* cleanup EQ
* context-based EQ



## Why it matters

Weak production often comes from:

* boosting everything for excitement
* EQing sounds in solo
* piling up low and low-mid energy
* confusing brightness with clarity
* solving arrangement problems only with EQ

Strong production usually has:

* intentional spectral roles
* fewer unnecessary boosts
* enough separation for important material
* decisions made in context



## Hearing model



\### Frequency landmarks (practical)



Approximate ranges:



\- Sub (<60Hz): weight, rumble

\- Low (60–200Hz): body, fullness

\- Low-mids (200–500Hz): mud / thickness

\- Mids (500Hz–2kHz): presence, clarity

\- High-mids (2–6kHz): attack, definition

\- Highs (6kHz+): brightness, air



Use these as rough guides, not rigid rules.



### Frequency balance

A mix feels clear when important elements occupy compatible ranges.

This does NOT mean every sound owns a separate isolated band.
It means they do not fight constantly.



### Masking

Masking happens when one element hides another.

Common masking pairs:

* kick and bass
* vocal and mids-heavy synths
* snare and dense guitars / chords



### Cuts vs boosts

A cut removes obstruction.
A boost adds emphasis.

Most beginners overboost because boosts feel exciting in the short term.

But too many boosts:

* crowd the spectrum
* reduce headroom
* increase harshness



### Context matters

A sound that feels thin in solo may sit perfectly in the mix.
A sound that feels huge in solo may bury everything else in context.



## Production heuristics

* Cut before boosting when solving conflicts
* Listen in context first
* Remove lows from elements that do not need bass weight
* Be suspicious of “air” boosts when the real issue is masking below
* Use EQ to support roles, not to force every sound to dominate
* If the mix feels muddy → reduce low-mids before boosting highs
* If something lacks clarity → check masking before boosting presence
* If something feels weak → check level before EQ



## Core drills

### Drill 1 — Solo vs context

EQ one sound in solo, then re-check in the full mix.

Goal:
hear why solo decisions can mislead



### Drill 2 — Low-end cleanup

High-pass or reduce low energy on a non-bass element.

Goal:
hear regained space in the mix



### Drill 3 — Masking contrast

Identify two competing elements and cut one slightly where the other matters most.

Goal:
hear separation without obvious tonal damage



### Drill 4 — Boost restraint

Try to solve one problem first with a cut, then with a boost.

Goal:
hear why cuts are often cleaner



## Contrast prompts

Ask:

* what is being masked by this sound?
* is this really dull, or just crowded?
* did the mix get clearer or only brighter?
* does this EQ move help in context?



## Common mistakes

* EQing too much in solo
* boosting lows and highs everywhere
* forgetting that arrangement affects spectrum
* making narrow surgical moves without hearing the actual problem
* using EQ to compensate for weak sound selection
