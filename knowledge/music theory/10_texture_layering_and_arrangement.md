# Texture, Layering, and Arrangement



\## Core idea

Composition is not just notes and chords.
It is also **how many things happen at once** and **how roles are distributed**.



\## High-value texture types

* monophonic
* homophonic
* polyphonic
* melody + accompaniment
* riff + support
* pad + figure + bass
* drone + event layer



\## Why this matters

Poor arrangements often fail because:

* every layer competes
* nothing has a role
* registral space is crowded
* density never changes



\## Register (Pitch Range)

Register is where sounds sit vertically (low vs high).

Low:

\- feels grounded

\- often used for bass

Mid:

\- fills space

\- often used for harmony

High:

\- feels prominent and energetic

\- often used for melody

The same notes in different registers feel completely different.

Good arrangements separate layers by register to avoid crowding.



\## Role-based thinking

Every layer should have a job:

* pulse
* bass support
* harmony fill
* melodic lead
* counter-line
* texture/noise/ambience



\## Core drills

### Drill 1 — 3-role arrangement

Write only:

* bass
* chord support
* melody

Goal:
hear role clarity

### Drill 2 — subtractive arrangement

Remove one layer from a full texture.

Goal:
hear what that layer was actually doing

### Drill 3 — density shape

Write 4 cycles:

* sparse
* medium
* dense
* sparse again

Goal:
hear energy contour

### Drill 4 — register spacing

Keep bass low, accompaniment mid, melody high.

Goal:
hear clarity from spacing



\## Contrast prompts

Ask:

* which layer is the focus?
* does removing one part improve clarity?
* where is the texture too crowded?



\## Common mistakes

* stacking all parts in the same range
* writing every layer as equally important
* no density contrast across sections

Mute melody:

→ does structure still hold?
